ENERGY TRANSITION / PHYSICAL CLIMATE RISK

When the Climate Outgrows the Crop

Every plant has a temperature envelope and a chilling-hour budget. I have mapped Himachal's full produce portfolio against its crop hardiness requirements and the state's projected warming. The result is a clear ranking of what the climate will break first, what it will not break, and what it will quietly favour.

BY THERESA RONNIE, FOUNDER · BOMBAY BREED APRIL 2026 · 2,900 WORDS
The Signal

Himachal Pradesh's climate modelling projects an annual mean temperature increase of 1.3 to 1.9 degrees Celsius for 2021-2050 against the 1971-2000 baseline, with a larger rise in minimum temperatures (Report on Climate Modelling, HP Department of Environment, Science and Technology). Under IPCC high-emission scenarios, India's near-term warming is projected at 0.5 to 2 degrees Celsius by 2049. CMIP6 projections specific to Himachal indicate that heatwave frequency and intensity increase with altitude, contrary to the assumption that higher elevations are protected (Rana et al., Environmental Quality Management, 2025). Apple requires 1,000 to 1,600 chilling hours below 7 degrees Celsius to fruit. Tea yields fall 4 percent for every 1 degree above 28 degrees Celsius. Saffron requires 1,000 to 1,200 chilling hours below 4 degrees Celsius for vernalisation. These are not adjustable parameters.

Theresa's View

The Himalayan climate story is often told as one of retreating snow and melting glaciers. That framing is correct but incomplete. The economic mechanism by which warming converts into livelihood loss runs through crop hardiness: the non-negotiable temperature and chilling requirements that each species evolved to need. When a warming trajectory crosses a hardiness threshold, the crop does not yield a little less. It stops yielding usefully.

Himachal Pradesh is a useful test case because its produce portfolio spans almost the entire range of temperate and subtropical agriculture in a single state: apples that need nearly freezing winters, saffron that needs high-altitude cold deserts, tea that needs temperate humidity, off-season vegetables that exploit the very specific summer window when the plains cannot grow them, livestock systems that follow transhumance patterns tied to alpine meadow cycles. Every one of these systems has a hardiness limit that is now being tested.

I have cross-linked each major produce category from our earlier map against three things: its chilling hour or temperature requirement, Himachal's current position relative to that requirement, and the projected shift by 2030 and 2050. The matrix below is deliberately unsparing. It is not a forecast of catastrophe. It is an honest mapping of which parts of the produce economy are already at or past their hardiness threshold, which are in the transition zone, and which have headroom.

The Framework
How climate intersects a crop's hardiness envelope

Three parameters determine whether a produce category is climate-resilient, at threshold, or in collapse.

01
Chilling budget
The hours below a species-specific temperature (typically 7 degrees Celsius for temperate fruits, 4 degrees for saffron) required to break dormancy and flower. Below threshold means no flowers, no fruit set, no yield.
02
Thermal ceiling
The upper temperature above which photosynthesis stalls, pollen desiccates, or yield drops proportionally. Tea loses 4 percent yield per 1 degree Celsius above 28. Apples desiccate pollen at flowering above 30.
03
Moisture synchrony
The match between rainfall timing and crop phenology. Winter snow for orchards, monsoon rhythm for vegetables, dry harvests for heeng and kala zeera. When monsoon and Western Disturbance timing shift, synchrony breaks.

The key scientific point is that these three parameters operate as hard floors and ceilings, not smooth gradients. A small excursion past threshold does not produce a proportional yield drop. It produces a step change. This is why the apple belt did not gradually thin in lower Kullu and Shimla over two decades; it collapsed, while new planting migrated 1,000 metres uphill into Lahaul-Spiti. The underlying signal is sharp even when the aggregate numbers look smooth.

What Others Miss / Four Exposure Tiers

The matrix above collapses into four operationally useful tiers. These are not a ranking of which crops I like; they are a ranking of how much climate adaptation capital each segment requires, and over what timescale.

Tier 01
At or past threshold
Apple (lower belt). Trout (lower streams). Segments of the walnut and cherry belts.
The crops already breaking

The lower-elevation apple belt in Solan, Sirmaur and lower Kullu has effectively lost commercial viability. Traditional varieties needing 1,000 to 1,600 chilling hours cannot accumulate them at elevations below 1,500 metres. Yield losses of 25 to 40 percent in traditional belts, with production migrating 1,000 metres upslope into Lahaul-Spiti. Trout farms in Kullu, Mandi and Chamba report summer stream temperatures approaching the 18 degree Celsius thermal stress threshold, with lethal conditions above 22. The investment implication is that defending these crops in their current locations is not the right strategy. Capital should be routed to upslope relocation, low-chill varietal substitution, and alternative uses for former orchard land.

RELOCATION-FIRST OR SUBSTITUTION-FIRST. NOT DEFEND-IN-PLACE.
Tier 02
Approaching threshold by 2050
Off-season vegetables, Kangra tea, dairy, most stone fruits, pashmina, alpine medicinal plants.
The crops where adaptation still works

This tier represents the bulk of Himachal's produce economy by value. Off-season vegetables (Rs 16,076 crore annual output) depend on a summer window that is compressing at both ends as the plains stay hot later into autumn and Himachal's summer heats earlier. Kangra tea is already measuring yield loss at every degree above the 28 degree Celsius ceiling. Dairy production faces heat stress compression of 8 to 15 percent without intervention. Alpine medicinal plant species face range loss estimated at 20 to 35 percent by 2050 (ICAR and CSIR-IHBT literature). The investment implication is that adaptation pathways exist but require active management. Shade netting, drip irrigation, heat-tolerant cultivars, shift to high-density planting, housed dairy systems, and protected cultivation of medicinal species.

ADAPT-IN-PLACE WITH SIGNIFICANT CAPEX. REQUIRES STATE AND PRIVATE INVESTMENT.
Tier 03
Partial exposure, manageable
Seed potato, cereals, saffron pilots, Himachali kala zeera, heeng, hops.
The crops that migrate within the state

These crops have partial exposure at lower elevations but viable refugia at higher ones. Seed potato production is already consolidating into Lahaul-Spiti and upper Kinnaur. Saffron pilots in Kinnaur and Bharmour (above 2,000 m) meet the 1,000 to 1,200 chilling hour threshold; lower-elevation pilots in Palampur and Kullu do not. Himachali kala zeera, heeng and hops are native or well-suited to the trans-Himalayan cold desert belt and remain viable through 2050 under moderate warming. The investment implication is that these are the crops state agricultural policy should actively promote in the diversification portfolio, because they are genuinely climate-compatible with Himachal's future.

FAVOUR INTERNAL MIGRATION. POLICY CAN CHANNEL CROP SHIFTS UPSLOPE.
Tier 04
Net beneficiaries
Citrus, mango and subtropical fruits in lower belts. Industrial hemp. Low-chill fruit varieties.
The crops where warming is a tailwind

Not every produce category loses. Citrus, mango and subtropical fruits in Kangra, Una and Bilaspur are currently in their optimal thermal envelope, and warming expands their viable zone upslope into former apple territory. Industrial hemp, legalised in September 2024 with a pilot licensing framework in 2026, has one of the widest thermal envelopes of any commercial crop and is genuinely climate-resilient. Low-chill fruit varieties (under 400 chilling hours) can replace high-chill apple and stone fruit in mid-elevation belts. The investment implication is that Himachal's diversification play is not just defensive. There is a genuine upside in the low-elevation subtropical expansion and in hemp, both of which map directly to the climate trajectory rather than fighting it.

How to read this portfolio under climate stress

Three observations compress the analysis into something boards and investors can actually act on.

First, Himachal is not losing its produce economy. It is losing its current produce geography. Every crop category still exists somewhere in the state at 2050. Apples still exist, just 1,000 metres higher. Trout still exists, just in colder tributaries. Alpine medicinal plants still exist, just with smaller ranges. The planning challenge is not "how do we save the apple" but "how do we manage an orderly climate-driven migration of the produce economy". This is an infrastructure, land-use and capital-deployment problem, not an agricultural one.

Second, the single-commodity story is the wrong story. Apple dominates the narrative because it dominates the headline economics and the political voice of the state. But the matrix above shows that the climate-resilient segments (saffron, heeng, hops, kala zeera, hemp, medicinal plants, low-elevation subtropical fruits) collectively represent a larger theoretical Rs-per-hectare opportunity than the incremental defence of apple at lower elevations. The HP State Cabinet's January 2025 cannabis pilot approval and the ongoing CSIR-IHBT aromatic crops mission are precisely this strategy. They are not diversification against apple; they are climate repositioning of the whole produce portfolio.

Third, the crops in Tier 02 are where policy can still make a material difference. Tier 01 is largely a transition problem. Tier 04 will adapt itself given licensing and market linkages. Tier 02, the off-season vegetables, tea, dairy and stone fruits, sits on the decision margin where incremental adaptation capital (shade structures, cold chain, protected cultivation, heat-tolerant livestock breeds, irrigation storage, pest and disease surveillance) translates directly into preserved yield and preserved livelihoods. This is where the state's climate adaptation budget and development finance institutional capital should concentrate.

The broader implication for Indian climate investment

Himachal is a microcosm of what will play out across India's entire temperate and subtropical agriculture belt. The Kashmir saffron industry, the Darjeeling tea industry, Uttarakhand's apple and pear belt, the Nilgiris tea estates, and Sikkim's large cardamom all face versions of the same crop-hardiness squeeze. The investors and DFIs who build frameworks now for evaluating agri-climate exposure at the crop-hardiness level, rather than at the broad sector level, will price risk more accurately than those still using aggregated GDP figures.

That framework is what I have tried to lay out above. It is deliberately unsentimental about which crops can be defended and which cannot. It is equally deliberate about identifying the categories that are genuinely climate-resilient and under-invested. The Himachal Pradesh produce economy through 2050 will be smaller in some categories and larger in others. Whether it ends up net expanded or net compressed is a function of how well the policy and capital decisions of the next decade match the hardiness map.
