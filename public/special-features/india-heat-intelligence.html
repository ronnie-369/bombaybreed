01 / The Definition

When does heat become a heatwave.

In atmospheric science, the term has a precise threshold with sub-thresholds, geographic modifiers, and persistence rules. The framework below is verbatim from the India Meteorological Department's official Heat Wave Definition document.

Threshold 01

The Floor

A heatwave can be considered only when daytime maximum temperature reaches at least 40°C in plains, 30°C in hill regions, or 37°C at coastal stations. Below these floors, no heatwave can be declared regardless of other anomalies.

Threshold 02

The Persistence Rule

Conditions must be met at a minimum of two stations in a meteorological sub-division for at least two consecutive days. The heatwave is declared on the second day. A single hot afternoon, however severe, is not a heatwave.

Threshold 04 / The Underdiscussed Metric

Warm Nights

A warm night is declared only when daytime maximum is 40°C or more, and the night meets these criteria. The body recovers from heat at night. When this window closes, cardiovascular mortality compounds. This is the metric the IMD uses to flag escalating health risk, and the metric mortality researchers prioritise.

Why this matters / Forecast skill versus event magnitude

The IMD's seasonal outlook for April to June 2026 (issued 31 March) was directionally correct: it forecast above-normal heatwave days for parts of east, central and northwest India. The Extended Range outlook on 02 April was an explicit miss at the two-week horizon. This is not a critique of the IMD's modelling capability. It is a documented illustration of the gap between current numerical weather prediction skill and the magnitude of events that the warming Indian climate is now producing. Investment decisions, agricultural insurance, power-grid planning, and health-system preparedness all depend on this forecast skill. The 25-day gap between "no heatwave" and "severe heatwave" is the operational reality those systems must absorb.

The 47.4°C station / Banda District

Banda sits at the geographic centre of the Bundelkhand heat plume.

Banda district lies in the Bundelkhand region of southern Uttar Pradesh, on the Yamuna river plain, bordered by Madhya Pradesh to the south. Kanpur sits 200 km to the north along the AH1 corridor; Chitrakoot and Mahoba sit immediately south and southwest. It is one of the most consistently heat-stressed districts in India, with semi-arid climate, sparse forest cover, and limited groundwater. The district recorded 47.4°C on 27 April 2026, crossing the IMD Severe Heatwave absolute threshold of 47°C, twenty-five days after the IMD's Extended Range Outlook said no heatwave conditions were likely.

Bundelkhand has appeared repeatedly in heat-stress and agrarian-distress literature. Its position in the geographic centre of the central Indian heat corridor makes it the natural canary for the eastern shift in India's warming trend.

06 / The Twenty-Year Curve

The plume has shifted east. The 2026 event is the trend made visible.

The Nature paper compares two consecutive twenty-year periods, 1981 to 2000 and 2001 to 2020. Across the boundary, the geography of India's warming has shifted. Strongest warming in the earlier period sat in the south and west. Strongest warming in the recent period has migrated into Peninsular and Eastern India. What Tmax is. Tmax is the daily maximum surface air temperature, measured at standard meteorological screen height (two metres above ground). 08 / The Attribution

How much of this is climate change.

Peer-reviewed rapid attribution science on the 2022 India-Pakistan heatwave provides the cleanest answer available for the subcontinent. The headline number is the probability shift, not the temperature delta.

30x

More likely under today's climate than in a preindustrial baseline. The probability shift is the structural finding. The intensity lift was modest (about 1°C), but the change in event frequency is what compounds risk over investment horizons.

What it means / 01
Frequency, not severity, is the structural shift.

The intensity number (1 degree hotter) sounds modest. The frequency number (30 times more likely) is the real signal. A heatwave that would have hit once every 3,000 years before industrialisation now hits once every 100 years. In a stable climate, you would have waited thirty generations for the 2022 event. We just had two in twenty years.

What it means / 02
Heat does not kill in straight lines.

Mortality from heat doubles for every additional degree above the local survival threshold. A one-degree lift at the top of the temperature distribution produces far more deaths, hospital admissions, and labour-productivity losses than the same one-degree lift at average temperatures. So a "modest 1 degree shift" delivers a non-modest mortality response.

What it means / 03
The future arrives inside the investment horizon.

Under 2 degrees of global warming (a level the world is currently on track to reach), the same heat event becomes 2 to 20 times more likely than today. The 1-in-100 year event today becomes a 1-in-5 to 1-in-50 year event tomorrow. For investments with a ten to twenty-five year horizon, an event that was statistically rare becomes statistically expected.

Important caveat / Conservative bias

The Zachariah et al. team explicitly noted that their results are likely a lower bound. The relatively short observed data record makes it difficult to fit the statistical distributions ideally suited to extremes. Larger model ensembles indicate a larger increase in likelihood than 30 times. The actual probability shift may be considerably greater than what the peer-reviewed estimate captures.

Source: Zachariah et al. (2023), Environmental Research: Climate. World Weather Attribution / WMO rapid attribution study, May 2022. In 2015 alone, the Telangana and Andhra Pradesh heatwave claimed more than 2,500 lives. The event was triggered by a delayed southwest monsoon onset, the same risk profile the IMD has flagged for 2026.

Important caveat / Likely undercount

India's heat-mortality coding is inconsistent. A 2024 systematic review noted that NDMA records lockdown-era heat deaths at 4 in 2020, while EnviStats-India reports 27 for the same year. The discrepancy reflects definitional differences but also undercounting. Excess-death analyses consistently estimate the true India heat toll at 2 to 5 times the official figure. The 24,000 number is a floor, not a measurement.

10 / Economic Exposure

USD 250 billion at risk annually by 2030.

Three exposure layers should be tracked separately by investors and corporate boards: agricultural output, labour productivity, and the combined power-grid plus water-stress envelope.

Layer 01 / Agriculture
Boro rice, maize, pulses

Heat stress during reproductive stages causes poor grain setting, flower drop, and reduced yields across east, northeast, northwest, and southern peninsular India. Wheat and chickpea face accelerated maturity and reduced grain filling in east-central regions. The 2022 event delivered a 10 to 35 percent yield reduction in Haryana, UP, and Punjab.

Layer 02 / Labour
34 million jobs at risk

Outdoor and uncooled-indoor labour exposure projection by 2030. Construction, agriculture, MSME manufacturing, and gig delivery are the sectors carrying the highest stress. The economic translation is McKinsey-style heat-exposure loss, putting USD 250 billion annually at risk by 2030.

Layer 03 / Grid & Water
10 to 12 GW supply gap

Cooling load alone adds 50 to 55 GW to peak demand. Despite 65 GW of renewables added since 2024, an evening-peak supply gap of 10 to 12 GW persists. The Hindu Kush snow deficit means lower river flow downstream into the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra basins.

Source: IMD agromet advisories, April 2026; Business Today and Orissa Post citing economic loss projections, 26-27 April 2026; ICIMOD on Hindu Kush Himalaya snow cover; World Weather Attribution on 2022 yield impacts.
11 / The Bottom Line

The assessment.

Three forces converged: a structural warming trend, a cryosphere shock, and a synoptic setup. The IMD's 02 April Extended Range Outlook said no heatwave conditions were likely. Twenty-five days later, India held 95 of the world's 100 hottest cities and Banda crossed the IMD severe heatwave absolute threshold at 47.4 degrees Celsius. That gap is the climate signal pulling forward.

The trend

Tmax in eastern and peninsular India has risen 1.0 to 1.2 degrees Celsius across the recent two-decade window. Bihar, eastern UP, West Bengal, and Odisha now sit on the plume.

The trigger

Hindu Kush snow cover at minus 27.8 percent of the long-term average, the worst on a four-year record. A persistent Maharashtra anticyclone trapping hot air during peak insolation. Both stacked on the structural trend.

What comes next

May and June are typically the peak. The 2026 monsoon is forecast at 92 percent of normal, the lowest in thirty years. A Super El Nino is in the post-June probability cone. Watch warm-night metrics.
