What Himachal Grows: A Full Map of the Produce Economy

Fruits, vegetables, spices, tea, hemp, dairy, wool and trout. Mapped by season, by contribution to the state economy, and by what each actually does for people who buy it.

BY THERESA RONNIE, FOUNDER · BOMBAY BREED APRIL 2026 · 2,450 WORDS
The Signal

Horticulture contributes roughly 20 percent of Himachal Pradesh's state GDP, per the HP Economic Survey 2022-23. Apple alone accounts for 81 percent of total fruit production and 49 percent of area under fruit crops, supporting over four lakh families. Off-season vegetables generate annual output valued at Rs 16,076 crore, delivering net returns of Rs 60,000 to Rs 2 lakh per hectare against Rs 8,000 to Rs 10,000 per hectare for traditional cereals. The state recorded the highest wool production growth rate in India at 4.38 percent (Livestock Production Statistics 2025). Cannabis cultivation for industrial and medicinal use was legalised by the HP Assembly in September 2024, with the first pilot licensing framework expected in 2026.

Theresa's View

Himachal is often described as a single-crop economy. That framing is wrong. Apple is the headline, but the state's produce portfolio spans four agro-climatic zones from the Shiwalik foothills (350 m) to the trans-Himalayan cold desert (6,975 m), and includes high-value crops that most Indian climate and investment coverage misses entirely. Kangra tea. Kinnauri chulli oil. Lahauli heeng. Kullu trout. Changpa pashmina. Bunium persicum (Himachali kala zeera). A pharmaceutical-grade medicinal plant economy drawn from 640 native species. And now, a legalised industrial hemp sector that the state cabinet approved in January 2025.

The reason this matters is that climate risk is not distributed evenly across the portfolio. The apple belt is migrating upslope, losing yield at lower elevations even as it expands into Lahaul and Spiti. Off-season vegetables are vulnerable to the same unseasonal hail and monsoon excess that hit Kullu and Mandi in 2023. Tea in Kangra is exposed to rainfall variability. Trout farming depends on cold perennial streams that are warming. And crops like heeng, saffron, hops, cinnamon and CSIR-IHBT's aromatic basket have been actively introduced in the last decade specifically because the state is betting on a diversified portfolio rather than defending a single commodity.

I have tried below to map every significant category of Himachal produce, the seasonal window in which it comes to market, its contribution to the state economy where data exists, and its actual utility to buyers. The point is not completeness for its own sake. It is to show investors, policy readers and board-level climate risk teams what a serious diversification play looks like in a Himalayan state, and where the genuine commercial upside is sitting.

What Others Miss / The Full Portfolio

The seven categories below cover virtually every significant produce stream coming out of Himachal Pradesh today. I have included scale data where reliable figures exist, and flagged where numbers are partial or emerging.

01 · TEMPERATE FRUITS

Apple, pear, stone fruits, kiwi, walnut, almond

The foundational layer of Himachal's produce economy. Apple alone accounts for 49 percent of area under fruit crops and 81 percent of production, per the HP Economic Survey 2022-23. Shimla, Kullu and Kinnaur are the traditional belt; Lahaul-Spiti and upper Kinnaur are the new frontier as the climate envelope migrates upslope.

Scale
580,296 MT apple production in 2024 (Dept of Agriculture and Farmers Welfare). Non-apple temperate fruits add 27,911 hectares of area with pears, plums, peaches, apricots, cherries, persimmons and kiwis.
Season
July to October for apples. May to August for stone fruits. September to October for walnut and almond.
Economic role
Apple economy valued at roughly Rs 5,500 crore per year, supporting over 4 lakh families and 13 percent of state GDP. Direct and indirect employment for roughly 50 percent of the population in Shimla, Kullu and Kinnaur districts.
Utility
Nutrition (dietary fibre, vitamins, antioxidants), food processing (juice, jam, cider, apple wine), and emerging export markets. Walnuts and almonds as high-value nut protein and omega-3 source.
02 · CITRUS AND SUBTROPICAL FRUITS

Mango, orange, galgal, lemon, pomegranate, litchi

Grown in the lower hills of Kangra, Bilaspur, Una, Sirmaur and Solan districts. Citrus alone covers roughly 26,000 hectares; subtropical fruits cover another 56,976 hectares. Galgal (a large hill lemon) and Kinnaur's traditional citrus varieties are candidates for GI protection.

Scale
35,310 MT total citrus, 36,570 MT mango, 17,030 MT orange/kinnow, 12,181 MT pear, 11,256 MT plum in 2023-24 (MoFPI State Profile).
Season
June to August for mango. November to February for citrus. June to July for litchi.
Economic role
Second-tier fruit segment, important for Kangra, Una and Bilaspur farmers who sit outside the apple belt. Feeds processing industry (pickles, squash, juice).
Utility
Daily nutrition (vitamin C), processed foods, and local culinary traditions. Galgal is used extensively in Himachali pickling.
03 · OFF-SEASON VEGETABLES

Tomato, peas, cabbage, cauliflower, capsicum, beans, potato, cucumber

The commercial engine of mid and lower hill farming. From mid-June through September, Himachal is the primary vegetable supplier to Punjab, Haryana, Chandigarh and Delhi markets, because plains production is off. This single seasonal window delivers the highest per-hectare returns in the state.

Scale
18.04 lakh MT vegetable production in 2021-22, valued at Rs 16,076 crore annually. Tomato alone covers over 3,000 hectares in Solan and Sirmaur.
Season
Peas: June to September. Tomato: June to September. Cabbage and cauliflower: July to October. Seed potato: August to September.
Economic role
Net returns of Rs 60,000 to Rs 2 lakh per hectare against Rs 8,000 to Rs 10,000 for traditional cereals. Per HP government estimates, this is a 6x to 20x return premium over wheat or maize.
Utility
Food security for the plains during summer months when local supply fails. High perishability means cold chain and logistics are material bottlenecks.

04 · CEREALS, PULSES AND OILSEEDS

Wheat, maize, rice, barley, pulses, soybean

The subsistence layer of Himachal agriculture. The state is a deficit producer of foodgrains because only 10.4 percent of geographical area is cultivated and 75 percent of cropped area is rainfed. Kangra and Mandi produce most of the wheat, maize and rice; barley is concentrated in Shimla. Maize-wheat and rice-wheat are the dominant cropping systems.

Scale
Pulses at 49,000 MT in 2023-24 (MoFPI). Wheat, maize and rice together form the bulk of cropped area but output is small relative to national scale.
Season
Wheat: April to June (rabi). Maize: August to October (kharif). Rice: September to November. Barley: April to May.
Economic role
Low per-hectare value relative to horticulture, but critical for household food security for the 84.5 percent of farmers who are small or marginal. Policy support via National Food Security Mission (Rs 9.65 crore for 2025-26).
Utility
Household consumption, regional grain markets, and traditional food systems (siddu, babru, madra use local flour).
05 · SPICES, MEDICINAL PLANTS AND AROMATICS

Saffron, heeng, Himachali kala zeera, hops, ginger, turmeric, chulli oil, and 640 medicinal species

The most underestimated segment of the Himachal produce economy. The state hosts roughly 640 species of medicinal plants across its four agro-climatic zones, concentrated in Kinnaur, Lahaul-Spiti, Kullu, Kangra and Shimla above 2,500 metres. Himachal is the largest national supplier of Atish, Salampanja, Dhoop, Kutki, Bankakri, Chora, Daruhaldi, Talispatra, Revandchini, Vach and Somlata. Saffron, heeng and cinnamon cultivation is actively being introduced as import substitution.

Scale
Saffron pilot across 25 locations in Kinnaur, Mandi, Chamba, Kangra, Kullu and Lahaul-Spiti with 6,859 kg corms supplied so far. Heeng (asafoetida) pilot across 69 locations in Lahaul-Spiti with over 33,000 plants distributed. Aromatic crops (lavender, lemongrass, rose, rosemary, wild marigold, chamomile) on 1,691 hectares.
GI status
Himachali Kala Zeera (Bunium persicum), Kinnauri Chulli Oil, and Kangra Tea are registered under the Geographical Indications Act. These commodities compete on origin authenticity, not volume.
Utility
Ayurvedic and pharmaceutical raw material, international spice markets (saffron at roughly Rs 3 to 5 lakh per kg), natural dye and incense industries, and import substitution for heeng (India imports over 1,500 tonnes annually).
06 · TEA, HOPS AND EMERGING BEVERAGES

Kangra tea, hops for brewing, wine and cider

Kangra tea is one of only a handful of Indian teas with GI protection (registered 2 April 2012). Its orthodox black and green teas are prized internationally. Hops from Lahaul and Kinnaur supply India's craft and commercial brewing industry, a small but fast-growing segment. Apple cider and apple wine units are expanding, with a 70,000 litre per annum wine plant under construction at Jarol (district Shimla).

Season
Kangra tea: March to October plucking. Hops: August harvest.
Utility
Specialty tea export, brewing inputs for Indian craft beer (a category growing faster than mainstream beer), fruit-based alcoholic beverages, and value-added processing for apple gluts.
07 · INDUSTRIAL HEMP (NEWLY LEGALISED)

Cannabis for fibre, bio-composites, medical and nutraceutical use

The HP Assembly passed a resolution in September 2024 amending NDPS rules to permit controlled cultivation of cannabis for industrial and medicinal use. The cabinet approved a pilot study in January 2025, jointly assigned to CSK HP Agriculture University, Palampur and Dr YS Parmar University of Horticulture, Nauni. The pilot will identify strains, best practices and licensing framework. A state-level regulatory agency and seed bank are in design. Chief Minister Sukhu has publicly noted industry interest from textiles, bio-composites, pharmaceuticals and eco-construction firms.

Status
Licensing framework expected in 2026. Industrial hemp will be low-THC strains. Medicinal cannabis will be tightly regulated, similar to opium under NDPS.
Season
Typical industrial hemp cycle: sowing July to August, harvest September to October.
Economic role
Not yet quantified. Uttarakhand's parallel hemp programme has struggled with market linkages and regulatory bottlenecks. Himachal's research-led rollout is designed to avoid those mistakes.
Utility
Hemp fibre for textiles and rope, hempcrete and bio-composites for eco-construction, hemp seed and oil for nutraceuticals, cannabinoid-based pharmaceuticals. Low water requirement and high carbon sequestration per hectare make it a genuinely climate-positive crop.
08 · LIVESTOCK, DAIRY, WOOL AND FISHERIES

Milk, Gaddi wool, pashmina, meat, poultry, trout

The animal husbandry economy supports migratory pastoralism (Gaddi, Bakarwal, Gujjar communities) and sedentary dairy farming. Himachal recorded the highest wool production growth rate in India at 4.38 percent in the most recent Livestock Production Statistics (2025). Trout farming has emerged as a flagship cold-water aquaculture sector, with 600 km of riverine length stocked and a production target of 950 MT.

Scale
Milk: 17.49 lakh MT (2023-24). Eggs: 875.55 lakh numbers. Meat: 5,500 MT. Fish: 17,000 MT (2022-23). Trout: 568 MT valued at Rs 25.58 crore in 2018-19, trending toward 685 MT.
Breeds and segments
Gaddi sheep and goats (Chamba, Kangra, Kullu) for wool and meat. Chegu pashmina goats (Lahaul and Spiti) for fine fibre. Rampur Bushair sheep for apparel wool. Rainbow and brown trout in Kullu, Mandi, Shimla, Kinnaur, Chamba, Kangra, Sirmaur.
Utility
Wool for Kullu shawls, Kinnauri shawls (GI-tagged) and blankets. Pashmina for luxury textile markets. Milk for dairy and sweet production. Meat for regional markets. Trout for hotel and export channels, with a ready-to-eat smoked trout canning centre at Patlikuhal.

How to read this portfolio

Three patterns stand out when the categories are laid side by side. First, the dependence on apple is real but the state is actively diversifying. The rise of off-season vegetables (Rs 16,076 crore annual value), the GI-tagged spice and tea portfolio, the medicinal plants economy, the emerging saffron and heeng programmes, and now legalised hemp collectively represent a conscious hedge against a single-commodity collapse. Second, the segments with the highest per-hectare returns tend to be the ones exposed to the sharpest climate risk: off-season vegetables need reliable summer monsoon rhythm, stone fruits and apples need chilling hours, trout needs cold perennial streams. Third, the genuinely climate-resilient opportunities are sitting in the long tail: aromatics, medicinal plants, hemp, pashmina, saffron. These are precisely the crops the state is investing in through CSIR-IHBT, YSPUHF and the new agriculture university at Palampur.

For investors and development finance readers, the implication is that Himachal's produce economy is not a single-commodity story. It is a diversified portfolio being actively restructured around climate constraints. The next decade of state agricultural policy will determine whether the restructuring can keep pace with the warming it is adapting to.
