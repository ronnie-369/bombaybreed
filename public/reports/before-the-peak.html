Before the Peak

A super El Niño is forming. What it means for richer economies, and the window to act.

The United Nations put the odds at 90 per cent this week. For wealthy economies this is not a humanitarian story; it is a financial one, arriving through food prices, supply chains and insurance. It is also the most predictable major climate event on Earth, and there are eight months to prepare.

THE WARNING

"Arriving on our doorstep."

El Niño is arriving on our doorstep in the coming months with 90 per cent certainty. Impacts will hit even harder, travel even farther, and cross borders with devastating speed.
António Guterres, UN Secretary-General  ·  2 June 2026

The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) confirmed the onset on 2 June. It puts the chance of El Niño at 80 per cent for June to August and at or above 90 per cent after. Beneath the surface, the tropical Pacific is already running 6°C above average, a reservoir of heat waiting to rise. The United States agency NOAA gives a roughly 60 per cent chance the event reaches strong by autumn, and around one in three that it becomes a super El Niño, the rarest and most disruptive class. The peak is expected between November 2026 and February 2027.

What is El Niño?

A warm patch of Pacific that moves the whole world's weather.

El Niño is the warm phase of the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), the most powerful natural climate cycle on the planet. In a normal year, Pacific trade winds push warm surface water westward towards Asia and let cold, nutrient-rich water rise off South America. During an El Niño those winds slacken, the warm water slides back east, and the heat it releases reorders rainfall and temperature far beyond the Pacific. It recurs every two to seven years, lasts around nine to twelve months, and peaks between November and February. Its cool opposite is La Niña.

Traditionally. Peruvian fishermen named it centuries ago. A warm current that arrived around Christmas and drove the anchovies away was christened El Niño, Spanish for "the boy", after the Christ child. For most of its history it was treated as a local Pacific quirk. Only in the 1960s did the meteorologist Jacob Bjerknes connect the warm ocean to the atmospheric Southern Oscillation and reveal it as a planet-scale engine of weather.

THE SCALE

Four times in seventy years, and it moves markets.

A super El Niño is the top of the scale: a sea-surface anomaly of 2.0°C or more in the central Pacific. Since 1950, only four events have reached it, and every one left a mark on people, the planet and prices.

Strength is read off the Oceanic Niño Index, the three-month temperature anomaly in the Niño 3.4 region. The 2015 to 2016 event set the record at 2.6°C. Model runs collected by Carbon Brief now place the 2026 median near 2.2°C, inside super territory, though the true peak will not be settled until the autumn.

EXPOSURE

For a richer economy, this is a financial event.

The direct physical hit to the United States and Europe from El Niño is real but mixed, and sometimes even favourable. The exposure that matters lands on portfolios and price indices, through three channels: food inflation, supply chains and insurance.

Start with the surprise. In 1997 to 1998, one of the strongest events on record, the United States came out ahead. A milder winter cut heating bills and a quiet Atlantic hurricane season spared the coasts, for an estimated net benefit of around 19 billion US dollars against 4 billion in losses. A super El Niño does not flatten a large, diversified economy. The peer-reviewed evidence finds that big economies can roughly net out while small, agriculture-heavy ones bear concentrated, lasting losses.

That is the cushion. The bite is elsewhere, and it is growing. The United States has logged 403 billion-dollar weather disasters since 1980, costing more than 2.9 trillion dollars, and the annual count has tripled from nine to 23 over the last five years. Insurers including State Farm have retreated from California and Florida; modelling puts 6.76 million American homes beyond the reach of any insurer, and the January 2025 Los Angeles fires alone cost between 40 and 53 billion dollars. None of that is El Niño specifically, but it is the loss-and-repricing backdrop a super event lands on.

The clearest transmission is inflation. The European Central Bank estimates that a strong El Niño can raise global food commodity prices by up to 9 per cent, peaking around 16 months after onset. Bloomberg Economics finds past events added around 3.9 percentage points to non-energy commodity prices. That lands on Western shelves and in headline inflation just as central banks are trying to bring it down, which is why an El Niño is most damaging when it arrives at the wrong moment in the rate cycle.

The second channel is logistics. The 2023 to 2024 El Niño drought cut transits through the Panama Canal by around a third, lifting shipping costs into American ports. The third is insurance: catastrophe losses harden the reinsurance market and reprice premiums everywhere. For a richer-economy reader, the question is less "will I be flooded" than "what does this do to prices, cover and rates", and the answer is: enough to plan for.

THE WINDOW

Eight months is enough to cut the damage.

A super El Niño is the rare disaster that announces itself. The peak is still around six to eight months away, and the evidence is that acting on the forecast now, rather than reacting to the impact later, is the highest-return move available.

The numbers are stark. The Global Commission on Adaptation found early warning systems return more than tenfold, the best of any adaptation measure it assessed, and that 24 hours of warning can cut the resulting damage by around 30 per cent. The Food and Agriculture Organization found every dollar spent on anticipatory action returned more than seven in its best cases. A seasonal forecast extends that logic from hours to months, and unlocks the slow levers a short warning cannot touch.

The levers, ranked by what eight months buys

1

Lock in cover before it reprices

Insurance and reinsurance are placed and priced ahead of events. Parametric policies and catastrophe bonds, which pay out automatically on a rainfall or water-level trigger, can be arranged now, while capacity is available and before the market hardens into the peak. Stress-testing exposure today sizes that cover correctly.

2

Build storage and inventory before the spike

Pre-positioning food, grain and at-risk soft-commodity inventory, and front-loading European gas storage (which entered 2026 at its lowest since 2018) turns a forecast into a buffer rather than a scramble.

3

Pre-book supply-chain alternatives

The Panama Canal lesson is to reserve rerouting capacity and diversify sourcing away from drought-exposed producers before a draft restriction repeats.

4

Resource heat and health systems before summer

Europe's heat plans were activated but only partly effective in the 60,000-death summer of 2022. Standing up week-ahead heat-health warnings and disease surveillance is an eight-month job, not an eight-day one.

The honest limit

Eight months cannot retrofit a grid, build early-warning coverage where none exists, or change the rainfall outcome itself. It can only ready the response. Acting on a probabilistic forecast means some preparation will, in hindsight, prove unnecessary. That is the price of insurance, not a failure of it.

FEATURE · THE COCOA WARNING

The taste being engineered out of chocolate.

The same drought and disease that drove cocoa to a record price are also shrinking the land where it can grow. The industry's answer is to make chocolate with less real cocoa, or none at all. A simple pleasure is quietly being reformulated for the next generation.

West Africa grows around 70 per cent of the world's cocoa, and production there fell by up to 40 per cent between 2023 and 2025 under El Niño-linked heat, drought and the spread of swollen-shoot virus. Cocoa peaked at a record 12,646 US dollars a tonne in December 2024, more than double the previous high that had stood since 1977. Bean prices have since fallen, but retail chocolate has not: Mondelez says chocolate-category prices rose 30 to 50 per cent in two years.

The deeper problem is the land itself. A 2013 study in Climatic Change first projected that warming of around 2°C would push cocoa-growing zones uphill and render many lowland areas unsuitable by 2050. A 2025 study goes further, projecting that West and Central Africa could lose around half of their climatically suitable cocoa area by mid-century. Climate Central finds warming has already added around 40 extra days a year above 32°C in the cacao belts of Ivory Coast and Ghana, beyond the heat the trees tolerate well. This is the part that reaches the next generation: the crop may simply have nowhere cool enough to grow.

The response is to engineer around the bean

Two technologies are racing the shortage. The first is cell-cultured cocoa, grown from cocoa cells in bioreactors rather than on trees. In April 2026 Celleste Bio, backed by Mondelez, unveiled the first chocolate bars made with cell-cultivated cocoa butter, and aims to be market-ready by 2027; California Cultured is doing the same with cocoa powder. The second is "cocoa-free" chocolate, made with no cocoa at all from roasted sunflower seeds or oats. Planet A Foods' ChoViva now features in more than 120 products, and both Nestlé and Mars have run cocoa-free pilots, mostly in Germany.

Cocoa butter determines the texture, the melt and mouthfeel. There is nothing else in food that behaves quite like it.
Michal Beressi Golomb, CEO, Celleste Bio  ·  2026
What is true, and what is not

The claim that all chocolate makers have already switched to lab-made cocoa is not accurate. No cell-cultured cocoa has regulatory approval anywhere as of 2026, and cocoa-free products remain limited pilots; mainstream chocolate is still made from real beans. But the trajectory is set. In Britain, McVitie's has already relabelled some bars "chocolate flavoured" after cutting their cocoa content, and analysts now speak of real chocolate becoming a luxury. The pleasure that defined childhoods may, for the next generation, increasingly come from a bioreactor, or from no cocoa at all.

THE WINDOW CLOSES

From sporadic to scheduled.

For most of history a super El Niño was a shock that arrived without warning. It is now the most predictable major climate event on Earth.

The 2026 to 2027 event has been flagged eight months out, and the channels it will travel are already mapped: food prices, supply chains, insurance, energy. That foresight is the whole opportunity. The difference between a disruption and a managed risk is simply what gets done between now and the peak. The forecast is in. The window is open. It does not stay open.

The briefing in one line

A super El Niño is forming. For richer economies it is a price shock, not a flood, and the eight months before the peak are when it is cheapest to cut.
